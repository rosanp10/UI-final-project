Type 1

Monogamy🦢

Monogamy is when an animal pairs with a single partner, often for a whole breeding season or even for life. This strategy is common in birds — roughly 90 % of bird species are socially monogamous. Both parents typically share duties like nest building, incubation, and raising young, which increases offspring survival.

💡

Fun Fact: Albatrosses can live up to 70 years and often pair with the same partner for decades, performing elaborate courtship dances every time they reunite after a long migration.

Notable Examples

🦢 Swans 🐺 Wolves 🦅 Bald Eagles 🦫 Beavers 🐦 Albatrosses
Type 2

Polygyny🦁

Polygyny (one male, multiple females) is the most widespread mating system in mammals. A dominant male competes fiercely — through displays, roaring, or combat — for the exclusive right to mate with a harem of females. This drives dramatic sexual dimorphism: males are often much larger and more elaborately ornamented.

💡

Fun Fact: A male elephant seal weighs up to ten times more than a female. He defends a beach harem of up to 100 females — and most males never mate at all.

Notable Examples

🦁 Lions 🦌 Red Deer 🦭 Elephant Seals 🦍 Gorillas 🐄 Bison
Type 3

Polyandry🐠

Polyandry (one female, multiple males) is rare but fascinating. Here the female is often larger and more colourful, and the male takes on brooding duties. Role-reversal in mate competition means females compete for access to males. This is most visible in seahorses — where the male literally carries the pregnancy — and jacanas.

💡

Fun Fact: In the jacana (a tropical bird), one female holds a territory containing up to four males. She lays clutches for each male, who then incubates the eggs entirely on his own.

Notable Examples

🐠 Seahorses 🐟 Pipefish 🐦 Jacanas 🦀 Phaleropes
Type 4

Promiscuity🐒

In promiscuous systems both males and females mate with many partners. There is no lasting pair bond. Instead, competition shifts from displays and fighting to sperm competition — males evolve larger testes and produce more or faster sperm to out-compete rivals inside the female's reproductive tract. Bonobos use frequent mating to strengthen social bonds and reduce conflict.

💡

Fun Fact: Chimpanzees live in promiscuous societies where a single female may mate with every male in her troop within a few days. As a result, male chimps have testes roughly three times larger (relative to body size) than those of gorillas, who live in harems.

Notable Examples

🐒 Chimpanzees 🐵 Bonobos 🐬 Dolphins 🦟 Many Insects
Type 5

Sexual Selection & Leks🦚

Sexual selection is the evolutionary pressure that arises from mate choice. In leks, males gather at a communal arena purely to display and compete for female attention — no territory or resources are defended. Females observe, compare, and choose the showiest males. This explains why some animals evolved seemingly impractical traits like the peacock's magnificent tail or the bird-of-paradise's extraordinary plumage.

💡

Fun Fact: In the sage grouse lek, hundreds of males gather at dawn and puff their chests to create booming sounds. Yet fewer than 10 % of males account for the vast majority of all matings in a single season.

Notable Examples

🦚 Peacocks 🐦 Birds of Paradise 🐔 Sage Grouse 🦌 Elk (bugling)
